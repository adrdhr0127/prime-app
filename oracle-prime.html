The Psychology of Money — Housel
The Power of Compounding
Chapter 4
Buffett's net worth is $84.5B. $84.2B came after his 65th birthday. Good returns sustained uninterrupted will always beat high returns that can't be repeated. The most powerful investing book is one page with a long-term growth chart.
Never sell a compounder for a short-term gain. The highest-returning action is often no action at all.
02
The Intelligent Investor — Graham
Mr. Market and Intrinsic Value
Chapter 8 — The Investor and Market Fluctuations
Imagine a partner named Mr. Market who offers to buy or sell your shares every day at wildly varying prices. "If you are a prudent investor, will you let Mr. Market's daily communication determine your view of value? Only if you agree with him." Price is what you pay; value is what you get.
Graham Number = sqrt(22.5 x EPS x Book Value)
Buy when Price < Graham Number
03
Margin of Safety — Klarman
Risk-Averse Value Investing
Chapter 6
Value investing is buying securities at a considerable discount from underlying value. The greatest challenge is maintaining patience to buy only when prices are attractive. "Value investors have as a primary goal the preservation of their capital."
Three valuation methods: (1) NPV of future cash flows, (2) Liquidation value, (3) Comparable transactions. Buy at the lowest of the three.
04
Kelly Criterion — Shannon / Kelly 1956
Optimal Position Sizing
f* = (bp - q) / b — The Secret Math Formula
f* is the optimal fraction of capital to allocate. b = net odds (profit per unit if correct), p = probability of winning, q = 1 - p. This formula mathematically maximizes long-run wealth growth while avoiding ruin.
f* = (bp - q) / b
Half-Kelly = f*/2 (Klarman margin of safety applied to sizing)

05
Margin of Safety — Klarman
Institutional Constraints and Opportunity
Chapter 10
Institutional herding creates mispricings. "Lumbering behemoths trampling some securities to large discounts." Spinoffs, post-bankruptcy securities, index deletions, forced selling — where institutions cannot or will not go — is where Kelly + Graham produce maximum edge.
Systematically hunt where institutions cannot go. Small caps, spinoffs, forced sellers. These are where your mathematical edge is highest.
06
The Intelligent Investor — Graham
The Defensive Investor's 7 Criteria
Chapter 14 — Stock Selection
Graham's checklist: (1) Adequate size, (2) Current ratio greater than 2, (3) Earnings stability over 10 years, (4) Dividend record 20+ years, (5) Earnings growth 33% over 10 years, (6) P/E not more than 15, (7) P/B not more than 1.5, and P/E x P/B not more than 22.5.
Graham Number = sqrt(22.5 x EPS x BV) — maximum fair price
Margin of Safety = (Graham Number - Price) / Graham Number
07
The Psychology of Money — Housel
Getting Wealthy vs. Staying Wealthy
Chapter 5
Jesse Livermore made the equivalent of $3B in one day during 1929 — then lost everything. Rick Guerin used margin and was wiped out in 1973-74. "Having an edge and surviving are two different things. The first requires the second." The Kelly Criterion is the mathematical equation for survival-constrained growth.
Never size a position so large that a single loss destroys you. Kelly f*/2 (Half-Kelly) is the Klarman margin of safety applied to position sizing.
08
All Three Books
The Unified Framework
Graham + Klarman + Housel + Kelly
Graham provides the valuation floor (Graham Number, 7 criteria). Klarman provides the risk framework (margin of safety, three valuation methods, catalyst analysis). Housel provides the behavioral override (compounding, survival, Mr. Market). Kelly provides the sizing formula (f* = (bp-q)/b). Together they form an unbeatable system.
Only buy when: Price < Graham Number AND margin of safety > 20% AND Kelly f* > 5% AND a Klarman catalyst exists. Size at Half-Kelly. Hold with Housel's patience.
